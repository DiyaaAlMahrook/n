Why are solar panels so cheap?

Moore's Law

In the world of technology consumers have come to expect that technology goods will get progressively more powerful and cheaper with each passing year. In the early history of semiconductor manufacturing one of the founders of Intel, Gordon Moore, made the observation that the number of transistors on chip was doubling every eighteen months. That observation, now known as Moore's law, held remarkably steady for many years.

Since the number of transistors needed to produce a certain level of functionality on a chip is more or less constant, and getting those transistors onto a chip was getting easier and easier with each development cycle, one consequence of Moore's law was the fact that consumer electronics steadily fell in price from year to year for many years.

Swanson's Law

Dick Swanson was the founder of SunPower Corporation, an early manufacturer of solar panels. He also observed that solar panels were coming down in price over time. He suggested that this decrease in price was largely due to the manufacturing expertise gained by solar panel manufacturers over time. He proposed a rule, now known as Swanson's law, that said that the price of a typical solar panel in dollars per watt would fall by 20% with each doubling of the cumulative total wattage of all panels manufactured.

Unlike Moore's law, which represented a steady progression in time, progress on solar panel pricing operated in fits and starts as government incentives needed to stimulate production came and went at irregular intervals.
